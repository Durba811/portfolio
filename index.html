01
HP · India 2023–2025 Field Research Concept Validation

When the research said stop.

HP had a P0 mandate to enter the PVC card printing market. I had a field study that said the current solution would hurt the brand before it helped it.

Project image — add yours here HP · PVC Card Printing Research
The Situation

Brother owned the PVC card printing market in India — not through loyalty, but through the absence of competition. Jobbers (print shop workers who print hundreds of cards a day) were waiting for an alternative. HP's price point was right. The mandate was to move fast.

What I Found

Across 30+ jobbers in 5 cities, the appetite was real — but the design had a critical flaw. Fixing a card onto the HP carrier took more time and effort than Brother's workflow. In a shop where customers wait and switching costs are zero, that friction is revenue lost. A second phase of testing in Bangalore confirmed the problem persisted. One jobber shared a video: a Tier 3 city shop owner had already built his own workaround — and it looked like HP's engineered solution.

What Changed

I gave the team a clear recommendation: this product is not ready, and iterating on a flawed mechanism risks damaging brand trust in a market HP is trying to enter for the first time. The programme was paused. The roadmap was reconsidered. Stopping a bad product from launching is not a failure of research. It is research working exactly as it should.

"Stopping a product from launching badly is not a failure of research. It is research working exactly as it should."

02
HP · India & Global 2020–2026 Self-Initiated Mixed Methods 3 Design Awards

The question nobody had asked.

Hundreds of studies had been done on HP's control panels. None had ever asked what an ideal control panel experience should actually look like. I noticed that gap. Then I filled it — without a brief, without budget, with whatever resources I could find.

Project image — add yours here HP · Control Panel Research · 50–100 participants
The Situation

HP was entering a cost optimisation phase. The brief was to redesign the control panel for low to mid-range printers. I had identified earlier — through self-initiated research using surplus UserTesting credits, piggyback probes, and informal conversations with shop owners — that no one had ever defined what "good" looked like from the user's side. I was the only one in the room with an evidence base when the mandate arrived.

What I Found

Four design directions were tested across 50–100 participants. Hybrid touchscreen failed on durability and cost. Icon-only buttons created too much cognitive load. A thermostat-style dial confused users because printers occupy a different mental model to appliances. When two late iterations were too close to call qualitatively, UserTesting analytics on task completion and time-on-task resolved the decision — users could articulate preferences but not performance.

What Changed

The final direction: button-dominated, 2-line display, clear text labels, no icons requiring interpretation. Participants said it could be used by anyone — their children, their elderly parents. Production costs came down. The design shipped and was recognised at three international design awards, including the Singapore Good Design Award (2025) and the Red Dot Design Award.

03
Ola Cabs India & London · 2019–2020 Field Research Market Entry

Research that changed direction — twice.

In under a year at Ola, I ran field research that directly contradicted a product team's design rationale and led to a committed pivot — and then took full ownership of the company's first international market research, end to end.

Project image — add yours here Ola · Driver Research + London Launch
Part 1 · India

Ola's driver-partner app had a design rationale the team believed in. It had never been tested in the field. Across 15+ sessions combining mid-shift observations with in-depth interviews, I applied cognitive psychology frameworks to observed behaviour and found a clear pattern: drivers were developing workarounds, making repeated errors, and experiencing decision fatigue exactly where the product expected peak efficiency.

Part 2 · London

I took full ownership of Ola's London launch research — planning, budget, recruitment, logistics, discussion guide — for the company's first international expansion. The app had been built for India. London's context was entirely different: hidden payment structures, expat vs. local driver dynamics, pet-friendly rides, full upfront cost visibility. The research was right. Covid arrived before the iteration did. That does not change what the research found.

What Changed

In India: I presented the driver behaviour evidence to the product director and design lead. The team pivoted the driver app's onboarding flow. It was the first time I presented findings that contradicted a decision already in motion and walked out with a committed change. In London: the findings mapped to a clear set of design priorities for the next phase. The right foundation was laid, even if it did not get built.

"It was the first time I presented findings that contradicted a decision already in motion and walked out with a committed change. It was not the last."

04
Google · NBU India · 2017–2019 Foundational Research Cross-Cultural

Design for users you haven't met yet.

Google's Next Billion Users initiative asked: what does onboarding look like for someone who has never held a smartphone before? The answer required unlearning almost everything standard UX research assumes.

Project image — add yours here Google NBU · Tier 2 Cities, India
The Situation

First-time smartphone users in Tier 2 Indian cities, across varied literacy levels and languages. Google products had been designed by teams whose assumptions about users bore almost no relation to this context. My job was to surface the gap — accurately and without romanticising it.

What I Found

Three findings that changed the framing: visual literacy is not universal — icons the team treated as obvious carried no meaning. Social help-seeking is the recovery mode — users asked the person next to them, not the interface. And apprehensiveness is independent of capability — users who could complete a task hesitated because the cost of making a mistake felt too high. Permission prompts were the sharpest friction point.

What Changed

Findings were presented to the Google US team and added to the internal research repository. The research shaped the direction of follow-on NBU studies. More importantly, it shaped how I approach every cross-cultural study since: the failure is sometimes in the researcher's frame, not the product's design. Cross-cultural research teaches you to tell the difference — if you're paying attention.

"Sometimes the failure is in the researcher's frame, not the product's design. Cross-cultural research teaches you to tell the difference — if you're paying attention."
